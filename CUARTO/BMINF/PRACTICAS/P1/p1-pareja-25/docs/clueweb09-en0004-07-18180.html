Woman miscarries in hospital toilet

Posted January 9, 2009 12:09:00
Updated January 9, 2009 12:25:00

Another miscarriage: Nurses told the woman to go into the hospital's toilet. (ABC News)

  • Map: Maitland 2320

Another pregnant woman has miscarried in the toilets of a New South Wales public hospital, this time at Maitland in the Hunter Valley.

Jodie Whiteside says she was 14 weeks pregnant when her waters broke and she was rushed to Maitland Hospital's emergency department.

The case is similar to that of Sydney woman Jana Horska, who was 14 weeks pregnant when she miscarried in the toilets at Royal North Shore Hospital in September 2007 after waiting two hours in the emergency department.

Ms Horska's treatment triggered a parliamentary inquiry into the state's public hospitals.

Ms Whiteside has told Fairfax Radio a nurse told her to go into a toilet to provide a urine sample and she miscarried there.

"[They] pointed me into the public ladies toilets to which I had to dispose of my child and finish off the birth of this child in a public cubicle," she said.

The head of Hunter New England Health, Dr Nigel Lyons, says they have already apologised to Ms Whiteside.

Dr Lyons says the hospital did not provide the appropriate level of care to the patient and the matter is being investigated.

"Somebody who presents to an emergency department with a miscarriage is going through a very traumatic experience and it appears, from what I know at this point of time, that we haven't actually offered the level of care that we should have," he said.

"What's important in these cases is not just the medical care but it's also the emotional and supportive care. I think it's that side of things that it appears at this stage we could have done a lot better on."

The Acting Premier, Carmel Tebbutt, says she is upset that lessons from the Jana Horska case were not applied in this instance.

"Following Jana Horska's experience there was a serious investigation, there was a lot of work done, there were range of changed procedures introduced including early pregnancy assessment services," she said.

"Those services are in place, as I understand it, at Maitland but they clearly weren't in place at the time that Jodie Whiteside visited Maitland Hospital and it's just not good enough."
